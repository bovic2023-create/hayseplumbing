Slab Leak Detection: Three Technologies, One Confirmed Location<h2>
Finding a slab leak requires tools that work through concrete without breaking it.
Acoustic listening equipment amplifies the specific frequency range of sound produced when pressurized water forces its way through a breach in a pipe buried beneath a slab. The sound is too faint for an unaided ear to distinguish from ambient noise. The amplified signal allows a technician to trace the source to a precise location by moving the sensor across the floor surface and tracking increasing signal strength. Hot water leaks read differently than cold water leaks on acoustic equipment, which helps identify which supply line is involved.
Thermal imaging adds a visual layer. An infrared camera reads temperature differentials at the floor surface. A hot water leak below the slab warms the concrete above it in a pattern that spreads outward from the leak point. On the thermal image, that warm pathway is visible. Cold water leaks create cooler zones in contrast to the ambient floor temperature. Neither technology works perfectly in isolation, which is why we use them together: when the acoustic signal and the thermal image agree on a location, we have confirmation before making a single slab opening.
A pressure isolation test establishes which supply line is leaking. We valve off the hot and cold supply lines separately and monitor pressure in each. The line that drops pressure over time is the one with the breach. This information determines the repair approach before any access point is cut.

Water Heater Repair: Reading the Symptoms Before They Become a Failure<h2>
Every tank water heater gives warning before it fails, but the warnings require knowing what to look for.
Rumbling from the tank is sediment speaking. Mineral deposits that have collected on the tank floor pop and crack as heated water boils beneath them. That sediment layer also insulates the heating element from the water above it, forcing the element to work harder and longer to maintain temperature. Flushing removes the sediment and reduces the thermal load on both the element and the tank walls.
Lukewarm water that worsens over time, combined with short white plastic fragments appearing in aerator screens and showerhead filters, points to a failed dip tube. The dip tube directs incoming cold water to the tank bottom for heating. When it cracks or breaks, cold and hot water mix at the top of the tank. The plastic fragments are pieces of the deteriorating polypropylene tube itself.
A gas water heater where the pilot light will not stay lit has a thermocouple problem. The thermocouple is a small sensing rod in the pilot flame path that generates a tiny electrical voltage from the flame's heat. That voltage signals the gas control valve to remain open and supply gas to the pilot. When the thermocouple fails, no voltage, the valve closes and the pilot extinguishes.

Emergency Drain Service: Main Line or Fixture?<h2>
The first question when a drain backs up is whether you are dealing with a clogged fixture or a blocked main sewer line. The answer determines everything about the response.
A single clogged fixture: one sink that drains slowly, one toilet that is slow to flush. The blockage is local to that fixture's trap or the short branch line connecting it to the larger system.
A main sewer line blockage: sewage from a flushed toilet backs up into the bathtub or floor drain. Flushing the toilet produces a gurgling response at the kitchen sink. Multiple fixtures back up simultaneously or in sequence. These symptoms indicate the blockage is in the main line downstream from where all those fixtures connect. Every drain in the house leads to that main line, and when it is blocked, any water added at any fixture has nowhere to go but back up through the lowest-lying drains.
When this pattern appears, stop all water use immediately. Do not flush, do not run the dishwasher, do not start the washing machine. Call Hayes Plumbing at (833) 957-0980 and describe what you are seeing. We respond to main line sewage backups with priority dispatch.

Water Heater Replacement: Sized for the Demand, Installed to Code<h2>
Tank water heater replacement requires sizing by First Hour Rating (FHR), the number of gallons the unit can deliver in the first hour of use starting from a full heated tank. FHR combines storage capacity with recovery rate and is the correct sizing metric for storage tank units because it accounts for real usage patterns.
Installation requires attention to code requirements that vary by jurisdiction but include consistent fundamentals: the temperature and pressure relief (TPR) valve must discharge through a pipe that terminates within 6 inches of the floor, the pipe cannot be reduced in diameter from the valve port, and the end of the pipe cannot be threaded. In closed plumbing systems where a backflow preventer, pressure reducing valve, or check valve prevents thermal expansion from releasing into the street main, an expansion tank on the cold supply line upstream of the water heater is required.
